Hannah AlNemri, Lynelle Collo, Bob Emrich, Toni Vang

Interview with Bailey Berry

Librarian for Digital Conversion, Publishing, and Curation

Our interview with Bailey Berry was conducted on Thursday, February 26th, 2026 over the course of two Zoom calls. Bob was not present during the interview due to his class schedule conflicting with the availability Bailey had provided us. We were also slightly limited on time to conduct the interview due to Bailey’s own schedule. As Hannah worked with Bailey and is familiar with her job, Lynelle and Toni conducted the interview while Hannah took notes.
B: Bailey H: Hannah L: Lynelle T: Toni

First Zoom Call

L: So first question just as an intro, what does your job entail on a daily basis?

B: Good question. It’s a lot. It’s a large mix of things. So my full title is Librarian for Digital Curation, Conversion, and Publishing. So I would say primarily I digitize items in our archives and put them online, I manage our digital collections website via Quartex, and we also handle a lot of scan requests from external researchers, things like that. And then I also manage the digital preservation of our digitized and born-digital items, and we use a platform called Preservica for that. And then I manage our Institutional Repository, which is through Digital Commons. So that’s all of our in-house journals get published there, like electronic theses and dissertations, all of that material. And then also I manage a bunch of kind of random digital projects, usually that end up being more like oral history projects. So the year I was hired, I had to finish up a grant project that was an oral history project.

[Interview paused while B took call]

I finished this grant project that was an oral history project, and I’ve been doing a lot of oral history work ever since.

L: You mentioned scan requests are these student requests or like outside patron researcher requests that are being made?

B: They are more- well both, mostly external researchers. We also get a lot of requests from the university marketing, or like alumni affairs or that sort of thing… So yeah, the majority of our requests are for researchers who want to access the materials. But we also get some like internal, more like marketing requests or dealing with alumni events, that sort of thing.

L: And could you give us an example of a large project you’ve worked on? I know you said you’re currently working on an oral history project one, or you mentioned the oral history project. Is there any other ones that you’ve completed in the past?

B: Yeah, so a lot of the things I’ve done other than these sort of big, like grant projects or like oral history projects or more like large scale digitization projects, so. One of the first things I did also when I started my job was digitize the Mary Frampton collection and put that online. So… I guess many of the big projects that I take on are digitizing collections. So– And I think of them as large projects because I usually start by meeting with the curator of that particular area, so for the Mary Frampton it was Malibu history. And we set out, like- we have a project doc where we set out what we want to digitize from the collection because we usually don’t digitize the whole thing, why we’re digitizing particular materials or what we’re hoping to achieve by putting it online, who our target audience is.

And then that informs how we construct our metadata, what sort of schema we’re using if there are like, particular control vocabularies that we need to use. And then that also helps with different ways of like, thinking about digital curation. So usually when I put collections online there’s just like the materials, the metadata, and then usually just like a quick background sort of contextual statement about the history and then a link to the finding aid. But sometimes I add a lot more contextual materials.

So I just digitized an antiphonal, which is a choir book from like the sixteenth century and there was a lot I did about the history involved, and the different ways of sorting the pages… [06:40-07:12 audio cuts out & T asked B to repeat last part of answer] Sometimes I do a lot more research or sort of exhibit building in order to present a digital item, rather than just like, basic background context.

T: So how would you say your role fits in, sort of- the larger ecosystem of the institution and of- kind of the profession?

B: Well within the institution, so I’m in special collections, which is interesting because I think before I was hired, my position was not, it was like it’s sort of its own thing. But because I’m in special collections, a lot of the projects I do are in partnership with the other people in special collections. So we have the university archivist who’s also managing our Malibu historical collection. And then we have the archivist for the Church of Christ history, which is like another collecting area. And then we have our rare books curator/instruction librarian. So a lot of the work I do, like a lot of the projects that I get are because they’ve approached me about like “Hey can you digitize this collection?” or “Can I get your help with this project?”

And then with digital publishing or in like the institutional repository, sometimes I interact more with general collection folks who are working more closely with campus researchers. So a lot of the work I do there is with with making electronic theses and dissertations available online, which the general collection librarians are also like, cataloging those and putting them in the physical collection. Or we have a few named book collections like alumni authors or faculty authors, and I’ve done projects where I’ve made digital instances for those in our repository.

And then that line of work also brings me into contact sometimes with like, grants and research and funding, who sometimes help organize research projects. Like for example they put together an undergraduate research forum every year, and they make a digital instance for that that they host in Digital Commons Institutional Repository.

So yeah, I also help the university archivist preserve digital university history. So there’s the student newspaper called The Graphic and they come out with a physical and a digital version of all of their publications and I’m in charge of ingesting the digital publications and preserving them. That type of thing. And then you asked how it fits into the profession as a whole?

T: And you could interpret that like libraries, information sciences generally, or you could limit that to special collections if you wanted.

B: It’s an interesting question. I think, I guess maybe one way to interpret it is- it’s interesting more and more how so much of university archives are primarily digital and not physical. So it’s interesting to see how much my job and the university archivist job intersect. I feel like sometimes we’re both doing the same work. Or like, we just have to work together all the time. And I know that that’s a conversation that a lot of people are having is like, mostly now what people are collecting in institutions that are like actively creating records is digital stuff.

And then also I think my job bumps up against issues in like, access and putting things online and like, open access. Especially with the Institutional Repository, I feel like there’s a lot of conversation around the importance of making scholarship accessible, the relationship between universities or institutions and publishers. Yeah. And I guess just sort of like, more theoretically, the importance of putting knowledge online, and then, just like a lot of the issues around that with like, copyright or, I don’t know, privacy issues. Yeah.

And then you know, recently there’s been a lot of talk about how much AI bots are scraping content that’s open access, and like sort of practically like, crashing a lot of our websites because they’re like the traffic is so high, and then just like affecting our usage numbers. But then, you know, there’s like this larger conversation about- if things are truly open access like are we comfortable with AI bots using this content, or are we trying to limit that? So yeah, I don’t know. I don’t know if that answered your question, but that’s what came to mind.

L: That kind of goes into one of the other questions we had prepared, is- are you planning on adapting with AI within your workflow, or like incorporating AI within your workflow, or have you made any adjustments because of the issue of AI bots scraping and taking material that’s been digitized and open access?

B: I have not made any adjustments yet. I know that- like, so Digital Commons for example, who they’re like run by Elsevier- they have actively been trying to figure out how to filter out bot traffic, and I guess since I’m using them as my platform I’m sort of letting them deal with that for now. I know that people have made statements about AI use of their materials or even research that uses AI that gets published on their platforms but I have not looked into that yet, mostly because my position with that is a little bit more hands-off. Like, usually there are people at my institution who are really managing the publications and I’m more like, just helping enable them in publishing those journals or teaching them how to use the platform, or if they come to me with those issues, helping them think through the best way of managing them. So not yet, but I can see probably in the future I’m gonna have to come up with some policies.

And then yeah, I have used AI a little bit in my work, and I’m sure I will have to. Or- not have to, but I probably will? It’s interesting, so I’ve really only used it- we’re transferring to a new digital preservation platform called Preservica, and part of the ingest process is that you have to prepare these packages of the digital files. And then, you basically send a little note to Preservica that has a list of all of the files, what it should expect in each package, and just some, like some system metadata that- we’ve been linking Preservica to Archive Space which is a different collection management software we use.

But in order to let that link happen, you have to basically embed specific metadata with the digital files. And so you have to go in and make a text file listing all the files and then adding this metadata and so I used- I’ve been using AI to write just like really basic batch scripts to tell my computer to go in and like- write a list of files and embed this metadata in like .xml files basically. So that’s how I’ve been using it, but there are a lot of platforms right now.

Like JSTOR is managing like a trial about this, and I know Preservica is in like a beta stage of doing this, and I’m sure there are plenty of other ones out there to help you do a couple of things that are sometimes annoying to do at like the bulk level- write like basic metadata about materials, identify people in images, identify like, Personally Identifiable Information or PII - so like social security numbers, or things that you, for like legal reasons don’t want in your archives or like definitely don’t want published. And when you get like a large amount of digital files, sometimes- I mean, there are a lot of tools out there to help you find PII but people are trying to build AI tools that can do it better.

And I can think of a couple of like huge digital collections, you know like 20 terabytes that we have. And we don’t really know much about them beyond what the institution who transferred them to us had told us. So the university archivist and I have sort of started talking about trying to develop an AI methodology for like- writing basic metadata for these files or at least trying to understand like- what they are. But I haven’t actually started doing that yet.

L: You kind of touched on this a little bit, but that was more so on the aspect of the AI part, have you needed to develop any specialized skills or knowledge as a part of your position, and how did you learn them, or when did you discover that you needed them throughout your time there?

B: Yes. It’s funny- I feel like I’ve done a lot of learning on the job, so I- honestly I feel like every part of my job is something I’ve had to learn while working there. Even like, using our- so we have a really nice Capture One camera stand, which is like, really great. And what we get with like- we paid a lot of money for that, and we pay a lot of money for the software, and part of what you get is someone who’s like, on hand to answer all of your technical questions and then like offer a certain amount of training every year. So like literally someone flew out and like spent a couple days with me like, helping me understand the ins and outs of the camera and the software during my first couple of weeks. And I’ve had to do a lot of learning about institutional repositories. I took like a whole certificate course on the best way to manage them and develop policies. As part of my oral history work I had to learn how to use like a- I had to buy and learn how to use a really nice video camera, and like microphone equipment, and like a lighting rig. And I had oral history experience before the job but I’ve sort of kept up with best practices and different like, I don’t know, methodologies for doing the work and writing like, deeds of gifts and informed consent documents. That sort of thing.

Yeah. Yeah, I mean- funnily enough it’s been- other than like the camera which is very technical I feel like a lot of the skills I’ve had to learn are more like managerial or their like policy related. So like you know, I’ve used AI to write batch scripts to run you know, Windows command on files but luckily I was able to do that fairly easily. I feel like a lot of the stuff I’ve had to get good at is more like the soft skills of you know- how to respond to tricky requests or how to like- project management has been a huge one that I’ve developed. And yeah, I don’t know what else. It’s, you know, I knew about like audio digitization but Bob actually when he was working with me helped me think through and develop workflows for like cassette tape digitization, because when I started my job, like- the method that we had like, didn’t make any sense at all. I even like, have taken apart a VCR and like cleaned it and looked at how it works and- yeah.

So I don’t know. I guess- I learned some things during my degree that have been really helpful about digital preservation or writing metadata or managing digital files, but I would say like 80% of what I do in my work I’ve had to learn on the job.

L: So you said that you learned a lot within the MLIS program, because you are a UCLA alumni, correct?

B: Yeah.

L: You said around 80% of your workflow you learned on the job, but how much do you think this- the program- as we’re students currently within the UCLA program- how much do you think it actually helped you within your position?

B: It has helped a lot. I’m really glad that I did it. But I feel like, you know, I feel like I hear people express this about the program where you’re not necessarily learning like, specific technical skills? It’s more theoretical. Which is true, and I think maybe the reason why is that like the specific workflows you’re gonna be using is gonna vary depending on the institution where you get hired. Like you might get hired at a place that has a really nice Capture One camera, or you might get hired at a place that only has like an Epson flatbed scanner. Or, you know, the specific job that you get hired to do- you might come across some- like, I don’t know, you might have to manage all of these different things at once, and I feel like it wouldn’t be possible for any graduate program to try and prepare you for like any potential scenario.

I think what like, UCLA does a good job of is helping you think through a lot of the issues involved in the work and like the field and helps you to start thinking critically about how you would approach issues that evolve like the introduction of AI or- you know. Like, I was going to school when UCLA was in active- I don’t know whether it was a lawsuit or it was- started battling with Elsevier over like journals and how expensive they were and had like, stopped subscribing to like, Nature and major journals. And so I remember learning a lot about the importance or the theory behind Open Access and that- those conversations have been incredibly helpful in the work that I do now.

So yeah, and I mean, certainly like, I can point to- you know the metadata class is amazing. Like, I use the skills that I learned in there all the time. And I took a digital asset management class which has been like a lifesaver. I feel like a lot of the practical skills I learned in that class and just like, information about different forms of storage media and like, I don’t know- the raw materials that are made out of even and like, some of the issues around- like the pros and cons of picking a storage media has been really important. But yeah, I guess I just mean like, day-to-day the like, actual hard skills that I use a lot of them are the ones are that I had to learn later. But it’s like, even just knowing- even knowing where to look for the resources to learn those skills? I feel like if I hadn’t done the MLIS program I would have been completely lost, if that makes sense.

H: I’m gonna jump in real quick, because you mentioned earlier- I know at least when I was there you would go to like conferences, and would go to like, meetings, and took courses and stuff, so like- because so much of your job is kind of learn-as-you-go, or like, jump in and start figuring things out, like- how do you actively continue to learn to aid yourself in a job like classes, lectures, journals, trainings-

L: You mentioned that you got, like a certification, too.

B: Yeah, yeah, so the- well, I got that one, and that was Digital Commons had, like a course that you could take and giving you a certificate on being an institutional repository manager. But I also took through the Society of American Archivists, the- I got my digital archive specialist certificate. Which, have you guys heard of that program? You don’t know? Okay. It’s really great. You have to take a certain amount of classes in like, different areas. I forget now what the areas are called. But it’s sort of like, basic theory and skills, like emerging trends, like I think- I don’t remember. Anyway. You get to take a bunch of classes, you get to choose what they are, but they’re more practical like- how do you ingest digital files, how do you write policies for- or like a preservation plan for your institution, how do you do, like emergency preparedness. Or how do you- I think project management was one I took through that, how do you pick the right tool for any given workflow. So yeah, I did- I got my certificate in that.

And actually even before I was hired I had started that certificate program just, like to help the job search process. And there are a lot of- there are just a lot of training programs that are out there, like SAA has a lot of classes that are ongoing. And I know that the VRA and ARLIS- so the Visual Resources Association and the Art Research and Library Institution- something like that-

L: Society. Yeah, I’m familiar!

B: Okay cool! I think they have classes they do every year, and I think they kind of cover a lot of the same topics, like copyright in digital collections. They take you through, like digitization workflows, metadata I’m pretty sure, and then like, the basics of digital preservation. So yeah, I really just pay attention to, like e-mail lists, or I subscribe to e-mail listservs that have to do with digital preservation or that are run by, like particular institutions like SAA or- there’s like a DigPres listserv. And so a lot of the classes or talks or conferences I go to I usually find through those listservs.

But yeah there’s- there are so many resources out there. I feel like one of the great things about our profession is- you know, it’s all about making knowledge accessible to lots of people and sharing information, and I feel like a lot of the people in our profession really take that to heart, and publish their digital preservation workflows, or post conferences where everyone shares the digital tools they use. There are tons of websites where people list the digital tools that they use. So yeah, there are millions of resources out there for anyone who wants to learn.
