IS 270 Systems and Infrastructures

Hannah AlNemri, Lynelle Collo, Bob Emrich, Toni Vang

Annotated Bibliography

Conway, Paul. “Preservation in the Age of Google: Digitization, Digital Preservation, and Dilemmas.” The Library Quarterly: Information, Community, Policy 80, no. 1 (2010): 61–79. https://doi.org/10.1086/648463.

Although this journal article was written in 2010, Conway had anticipated the conversion from analog to digital media, and what that meant for the flow of digital information. It is obvious that even from the beginning of digitally communicated information, Conway mentioned the preservationist’s concerns of the consequences of the digital age. He further defines digital preservation as an act of “responsible custody,” and differentiates the concept from digitizing for preservation. Conway exclaims that digitization for preservation “creates valuable new products, whereas digital preservation protects the value of those products, physical or born-digital.” The work of digital preservation then acts as an investment in both the digital products you make, as well as in the "tools, operations, standards, and policies” that allow for digitization to happen and continue to happen for the long term. Conway then goes into some potential cons for digitization, including environmental, overall quality of digital materials, technical expertise barriers, and the lack of standardization of “nonbook” digitization workflows. The article concludes by giving some recommendations for these dilemmas, and reaffirming that digitization is an investment with value that needs to be maintained.

Faulder, Erin, and Laura Uglean Jackson. “Digital Processing: Exploring the Enigma.” The American Archivist 85, no. 1 (2022): 146–72. https://www.jstor.org/stable/48829101.

Faulder and Jackson examine the concept of digital processing in archival work, particularly in relation to the acquisition, organization, and preservation of born-digital materials. The authors argue that while traditional archival processing practices are already fairly well established, the field lacks consistent terminology, guidelines, and standards for processing digital materials. The article identifies four key issues shaping this challenge, including defining the scope of digital processing, tensions between minimal processing approaches and technical demands, inconsistencies in terminology across institutions, and the convergence of traditional archival processing and digital preservation. These issues are largely identified by drawing on discussions from the Born-Digital Archiving &eXchange (BDAX) community, and the overall development of the Digital Processing Framework. By analyzing these challenges, the authors highlight a broader gap in professional literature and standards archivists must navigate when working with digital collections. The authors emphasize that digital processing is not simply a technical activity but an evolving set of practices. Because born-digital collections require specialized tools, metadata structure, and preservation infrastructure, archivists have to continually negotiate how much processing is necessary to ensure usability while balancing institutional resources and access goals. Thus, professional dialogue to develop clearer frameworks and shared terminology to guide digital processing is a necessary process. This source is useful for understanding how archivists adapt established practices to emerging digital formats and infrastructures. It also illustrates the evolving technical and conceptual skills required of archival professionals as digital materials become an increasingly significant aspect of archival collections.

Joyce, Déirdre, Laurel McPhee, Rita Johnston, Julia Corrin, and Rebecca Hirsch. “Toward a Conceptual Framework for Technical Debt in Archives.” The American Archivist 85, no. 1 (2022): 104–25. https://www.jstor.org/stable/48829099.

This article introduces the concept of technical debt, a kind of metaphor in software development, which describes the long-term maintenance challenges created by earlier digital projects in archival institutions. The authors argue that many digital collections initiatives, including website platforms, digital asset management systems, metadata structures, and preservation workflows, become difficult to sustain as technologies evolve and institutional priorities shift. The authors, by going through three different case studies, demonstrates how incomplete documentation and past design issues can create increased and ongoing costs in labor, time, and overall infrastructure in archives and archival collections. One important concept they introduce is the idea of “documentation debt,” referring to the lack of sufficient documentation for digital systems and workflows. The lack of documentation leads to difficulties in future migration, maintenance, and access. Thus, the authors propose a preliminary conceptual framework that archivists can use to evaluate digital projects across their lifecycles and make more sustainable decisions about infrastructure and preservation strategies. This source is useful for understanding the hidden labor and long-term planning involved in digital collections work, highlighting how archival professionals must balance innovation with sustainability and digital repositories.

Noonan, Daniel, and Tamar Chute. “Data Curation and the University Archives.” The American Archivist 77, no. 1 (2014): 201–40. http://www.jstor.org/stable/43489591.

Despite being written more than a decade ago, the concept of data curation defined in this article remains crucial to librarianship today. Through their survey of 126 library institutions, including 116 university libraries, Noonan and Chute’s research of data curation questions the universality and standardization of data and its utilization across different fields. By talking to a variety of archivists and librarians, the authors were able to make some generalizations about the tools, concepts, and the overall long-term stewardship of research data. The authors define data curation as a broad set of practices which enable discovery, retrieval, preservation, and the reuse of digital data, including metadata creation, data management planning, and maintaining data quality over time. Their findings reveal variation across institutions in how data curation responsibilities are implemented, including different collection development policies, types of data collected, and the level of institutional support. The study also highlights the importance of data management plans, particularly in research universities where digital data must be preserved and made accessible. Although much of the article consists of survey results and responses, the study demonstrates how the archivists’ roles are expanding beyond more “traditional” records management to include digital infrastructure. This source is useful for understanding the evolving responsibilities of university archivists and the growing importance of digital curation skills.

Noordegraaf, Julia, and Anna Schjøtt. “From Preservation to Access and beyond: The Role of AI in Audiovisual Archives.” In Navigating Artificial Intelligence for Cultural Heritage Organisations, edited by Lise Jaillant, Claire Warwick, Paul Gooding, Katherine Aske, Glen Layne-Worthey, and J. Stephen Downie, 93–112. UCL Press, 2025. https://doi.org/10.2307/jj.24215718.11.

This article argues that AI intensifies already present shifts toward datafication, expanded access, and expanded user participation in archives and museums initially brought on by digitization. The article uses case studies involving the Netherlands Institute for Sound & Vision, most prominently examining the Institute’s Media Museum, to illustrate points of this argument. On the topic of datafication, the authors examine archives’ use of digital, automated, and AI tools to generate metadata for archival objects such as timecoded speaker labels and captioning, or to create altered versions of archival objects in the case of colorization projects. Also discussed is how born-digital objects such as social media posts increasingly come with baked-in metadata of their own. The authors argue these datafications, while in cases expanding access, blur the distinction between archival metadata, digital metadata, and archival object, transfer away archivists’ descriptive and decisionmaking authority, and raise questions as to the evidentiary function of digital objects in the archive. The authors examine Sound & Vision’s Media Museum, exhibitions of which extract data from visitors to be displayed back to them, allowing increased user participation in their own experience, but also surfacing concerning issues of agency in an increasingly datafied world. This article raises questions about how the archiving of digital materials, the activities of digital preservation, and the curation of digital objects and experiences transform archives’, archivists’, and archival users’ roles, possibly in disempowering ways.

Senchyne, Jonathan. “Between Knowledge and Metaknowledge: Shifting Disciplinary Borders in Digital Humanities and Library and Information Studies.” In Debates in the Digital Humanities 2016, edited by Matthew K. Gold and Lauren F. Klein, 368–76. University of Minnesota Press, 2016. https://doi.org/10.5749/j.ctt1cn6thb.33.
